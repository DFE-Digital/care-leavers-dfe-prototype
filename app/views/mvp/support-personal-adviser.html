Personal Adviser (PA)

Find out how a personal adviser can help support and guide you when leaving care.

What is a personal adviser?

A personal adviser (PA) helps you prepare and make plans for when you leave care. They must also continue to support you after you leave, to help you live independently.

Who gets one?

You have the right to get a PA (you’re entitled to one) if you:

  • are aged 15 to 21
  • have spent at least 13 weeks in care, in total, between the age of 14 to 16 (this needs to include time after your 16th birthday)

It does not matter if:

  • you have left care
  • are in custody
  • you are seeking asylum

This also means your care leaver status is:

  • former relevant child
  • eligible child
  • relevant child

PAs are for people living in England. Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland have similar people and teams that support care leavers.

When do I get a personal adviser?

Your social worker must introduce you to your PA before you become 18. This might be when you’re 16 or 17.

They must support you until you’re 21, or until your 25th birthday if you ask them to (opt-in).

How often will I meet with them?

Your PA must meet with you at least once every 8 weeks.

You might see them more often, depending on what support you need.

How often you see your PA may vary if you’re:

  • a young person seeking asylum
  • in custody (like a secure children’s home or young offender’s institute)

If you’re in custody

You might sometimes have phone or video calls with your PA instead. Having face-to-face visits may depend on other visits you have planned.

What does a personal adviser do?

Your PA must:

  • help you plan and make decisions about your future (using your Pathway Plan)
  • help you find somewhere to live that meets your needs (suitable accommodation)
  • help you understand what support you have the right to and get access to it
  • talk through any issues or worries with you and offer guidance
  • help you develop practical life skills (like shopping, cooking or budgeting)
  • help you get set up for independent life (like help applying for benefits or opening a bank account)

A PA is different from a social worker, who helps you with decisions around your care (care plan).

Issues with your personal adviser

If you have an issue with your PA or you have not been given one yet, talk to your social worker or someone from your council. It might be that your PA:

  • is not meeting with you often enough
  • is not giving you the right support
  • has done something to upset you

If you’re still not happy, you can make a complaint.
