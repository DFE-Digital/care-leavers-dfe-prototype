Finding somewhere to live

If you're not sure where to live when you leave care, we’ll talk through your options and what help you could get.

Suitable accommodation

Help finding suitable accommodation

Your local council must find you suitable accommodation if you:

  • are between 16 and 21 (or 25 if you’re in full-time education)
  • have spent at least 13 weeks in care, in total, between the age of 14 to 16 (this needs to include time after your 16th birthday)

This also means your care leaver status is:

  • former relevant child
  • eligible child
  • relevant child

If your status is qualifying child, you’ll only get this support if you’re in full-time education.

If you’re not sure, you can check your care leaver status.

What is suitable accommodation?

Suitable accommodation is somewhere safe and suitable to live that must:

  • consider your needs
  • consider your wishes and feelings
  • meet housing health and safety requirements
  • be affordable for you
  • meet any health or disability needs

It should not be too far from:

  • your work
  • your school or college
  • where your training is taking place

Can I see it before moving in?

You should be able to visit somewhere before you move in to make sure it’s safe and suitable for you.

What happens if it becomes unsuitable?

Your personal adviser (PA) or social worker must visit you when you have moved in to see how you are doing.

If you find your home is not safe or suitable, your council must help you find somewhere else to live. Whilst they find you somewhere else to live, you might be given temporary emergency accommodation.

Types of accommodation

Whether you’re ready to live on your own or not, there are many housing options for care leavers.

What's available to you will depend on your council. Your council may work with charities which offer options too.

Supported accommodation

Supported accommodation is where there are people around to help you develop life skills. It helps to prepare you for independent, adult life.

This is sometimes called ‘supported housing’ or ‘semi-independent accommodation’.

It includes supported lodgings, where a person or family rents a room in their home to you. These people are also called ‘hosts’.

This is a good option if you leave care at 16 or 17 and you’re not ready to live on your own yet.

Shared accommodation

Shared accommodation is where you live with other young people.

This is sometimes called ‘group houses’ or ‘shared houses’.

You have your own bedroom but share spaces like a living room, kitchen, and bathroom.

Specialist accommodation

Specialist accommodation is somewhere you can live if you:

  • are disabled
  • have a health condition
  • have difficulties with your mental health
  • are vulnerable (at higher risk of harm)

This is sometimes called ‘specialised support housing’.

If you live here, there are always support workers available to help you.

Living on your own

There are different types of housing that may be available to you, like council housing (sometimes called ‘social housing’) or a privately-rented house or flat.

Your council may move you up the waiting list for council housing if you have a priority need.

You’ll have to pay for household bills like:

  • electricity and gas
  • water
  • council tax

You may be able to get help with housing costs.

Staying with your foster family

If you're already living with a foster carer, you can choose to stay with them.

This is called a ‘stay put’ arrangement.

You and your foster carer must make this decision together.

Returning to your birth family

If it’s possible and it feels right for you, you can return to live with your birth family.

Your PA or social worker can help you with this decision.

Housing help for your situation

If you’re a full-time student aged 16 to 25

Your council must provide or pay for student accommodation during term holidays if you:

  • are a full-time student
  • are aged 16 to 25
  • have a care leaver status

You might also be able to get other education and training support.

If you’re in custody

If you’re 17 or younger and in custody, you’re ‘in care’. This might mean you live in a:

  • secure children’s home
  • secure training centre
  • young offender institution

When you leave, you may get help:

  • finding suitable accommodation
  • with housing costs

Support depends on your care leaver status.

If you’re seeking asylum

You’re an ‘unaccompanied asylum-seeking child or young person’ or ‘UASC’ if you arrived in the UK:

  • when you were 17 or younger
  • alone or without a parent or adult

Social services will care for you until you become 18.

When you're 18, you’ll ‘leave care’ and get:

  • help finding suitable accommodation
  • help with housing costs
  • other leaving care support

Moving areas

What happens if you move areas

If you move to a new area under a different council, the council that cares for you will stay the same.

Your council must still give you housing support, including their Local Offer for care leavers.

They may work with the new council to support you, but this is their decision.

Who is my council?

Use the postcode of where you lived when you went into care to find your council.

Help with housing costs

If you’re 16 or 17

If you’re 16 or 17, your council must pay for your housing costs, like rent, bills, and other housing expenses.

If you’re 18 or over

If you’re 18 or over, you’ll have to pay your own housing costs, but you may be able to get housing support.
