Coming to the UK

Seeking asylum

If you came to the UK because it was not safe in your home country, this is called ‘seeking asylum’.

You will need to claim asylum (ask to stay in the UK as a refugee).

If you are under 18

When you come to the UK, you will have an ‘age assessment’ to work out how old you are.

This is important because it means you will get the right help for your age.

If you are under 18 and arrive without a parent or adult, Children’s Services will take care of you.

People helping you might call you:

  • an ‘unaccompanied asylum-seeking child or young person’ or a ‘UASC’ for short
  • an ‘unaccompanied child’
  • a ‘migrant child’
  • a ‘displaced child’
  • a ‘young asylum seeker’

Claiming asylum

What ‘claim asylum’ means

‘Claim asylum’ is when you ask to stay in the UK as a refugee (and get your papers).

Some other words people might use for this are:

  • ‘making an asylum claim’
  • ‘applying for asylum’
  • ‘making an asylum application’

You can read more about how to claim asylum in the UK or read a longer information booklet for asylum applications.

Who will help you

An asylum claim can take a long time, but you will have people to help you.

This might include:

  • a social worker
  • a support worker
  • a legal adviser (also called a ‘lawyer’ or ‘solicitor’)
  • an immigration adviser (to help you with your asylum claim)
  • a translator (who speaks your home language)
  • an advocate (to explain your rights and make sure you feel heard)
  • a personal adviserƒ
  • a foster carer (if you live with a foster family)

If you’re unhappy with a decision

Appealing a decision

If you are not happy with a decision made by The Home Office (part of the UK Government), you can ask them to look at your application again.

This is called ‘appealing a decision’.

It can take a long time, but you might get a different result by appealing.

It is your legal right to ask for this if you want to. If you do, you must appeal within 14 days (2 weeks) of getting the decision.

What you can appeal

You can appeal an asylum claim decision (about whether you can stay in the UK).

You can also appeal an asylum support decision (about getting help if you are over 18, like money or somewhere to live).

Getting legal advice

If you need legal advice, you can ask your:

  • support worker
  • social worker
  • personal advisor (if you have one)

They will be able to help you find someone who can explain your rights or how to appeal. You can also call one of the free helplines.

You may be able to get extra legal help too. Find out more in support if you have come to the UK.

Getting help and support

Support you should get

As a young person seeking asylum under 18, your local council (part of the UK Government) should help you with:

  • finding somewhere safe to live
  • money for clothes, food and travel
  • school or college
  • finding your family
  • your health and happiness
  • learning English (like finding a course to learn English)
  • planning your future

It is your legal right to get support, but some of it will depend on your asylum claim.

When you are 18

Being ‘in care’

Children’s Services will take care of you if you arrived in the UK and you:

  • were younger than 18
  • were on your own (without a parent or adult)

They will find you somewhere safe to live. This might be:

  • with family you have in the UK
  • with a foster family (called ‘foster care’)
  • a children’s home (with other children and young people)
  • supported lodgings (where you will have adults to help you)

This is called being ‘in care’.

What ‘leaving care’ means

When you are 18, you will ‘leave care’.

Leaving care means you prepare for and start your life as an adult. This can be a challenging time, but you will get help with this.

More about what happens when you leave care.

Support for leaving care

When you leave care, you may get support as a ‘care leaver’ from your local council (part of the UK government).

You may get some help until you are 25.

What care leaver support you get depends on:

  • where you live
  • how long you have been in the UK
  • if you are in school, college or university
  • your asylum claim

You might hear about ‘care leaver status’. This is how the UK government works out what support you can get.

To find out your care leaver status, ask your social worker or personal advisor (if you have one).
