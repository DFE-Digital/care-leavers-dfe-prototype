What happens and when

When you leave care

You do not have to leave care leave care until you’re 18.

If you want to, you can leave care at 16 or 17. This is a decision that your social worker can help you with.

Before you leave care

You'll get support to start preparing early so that you feel more ready when you leave. This might start when you’re 15, 16, or 17, depending on when you leave.

Your social worker will help you plan your move out of care, or you may get a personal advisor to support you.

After you leave care

Your local council must support you until you’re 21, but there is support you can get until you’re 25.

Meeting your personal advisor

What is a personal advisor?

A personal advisor (PA) is the main person who will support you when you leave care.

Your social worker should introduce you to them when you’re 15, 16, or 17.

Your personal advisor must help you until you’re 21, or 25 if you want.

How they should help you

You’ll meet with your personal advisor at least once every 8 weeks to:

  • plan and make decisions about your future (as part of your Pathway Plan)
  • get access to support
  • talk through any issues or worries
  • get guidance and advice when you need it
  • develop practical ‘life skills’ (like shopping, cooking or budgeting)
  • get set up for independence (e.g. apply for benefits, open a bank account)

If you do not get a personal advisor straight away, your social worker should help you with these things.

Preparing to leave

You’ll have a statutory review meeting

You’ll have a long chat with a few people about your future, to discuss decisions like:

  • where you’ll live
  • whether you’ll start or continue education
  • whether you’ll do training or get a job
  • what support you’ll need

People at the meeting will include:

  • your social worker
  • your personal advisor, if you have one
  • other people that support you (like a teacher or mentor)
  • an independent advocate, if you ask for one (to explain things and make sure you feel heard)
  • your carers, if you live with a foster family
  • an independent reviewing officer (someone that makes sure things are done properly)

You’ll have a needs assessment

You’ll have a ‘needs assessment’ with your social worker or personal advisor, which will feel like a chat.

This is how they work out what advice and support you’ll need to be independent.

This will help make your Pathway Plan too.

You'll make a Pathway Plan

A Pathway Plan is a document that you work on with your personal advisor or social worker to prepare you for leaving care.

You should meet to review it at least once every 6 months.

You’ll use it to set plans and goals around things like:

  • where you’ll live
  • managing money
  • education, training or work
  • your health and wellbeing
  • contact with your family (if you want to)
  • finding your community
  • support you’ll need

Deciding where you’ll live

Your housing rights

Your local council must make sure you move to ‘suitable accommodation’ when you leave care. This means somewhere you feel safe and comfortable.

You may also be able to get help with living expenses if you have moved somewhere for education or training.

Find out more about housing support.

Living arrangements

You’ll work out where to live with your Personal Adviser or Social Worker.

If you’re not ready to live on your own yet, there are options to ease you into independent life.

To learn more about your options, read our guide on finding somewhere to live.

If you’re in crisis, read our guide on facing homelessness.

Getting support

Support you should get

Leaving care can be a challenging time, so your local council must support you in different areas of your life.

What support you could get depends on your ‘care leaver status’, which is based on the time you spent in care.

Check your care leaver status.

Your Local Offer

Your local council will offer you extra support through their Local Offer. This might include things like Council Tax discount or driving lessons.

Different councils offer different things, so go to your local council’s website to find their Local Offer.

Check who your local council is.

Issues leaving care

Having your voice heard

When leaving care, it’s important you feel heard when making decisions about your life.

If you do not feel heard, you have the right to get an independent advocate to:

  • help you have your say in decisions about your life
  • explain things to you (like your rights)
  • make sure you’re treated fairly
  • make a complaint

Making a complaint

Before making a complaint, if you can, speak to your social worker or personal advisor first to see if they can help.

You have the right to make a complaint if you:

  • did not get the support you should have had
  • are unhappy with decisions made around your life
  • have been treated poorly or unfairly
